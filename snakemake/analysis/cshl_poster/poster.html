Data curation strategies for genomic language models

Gonzalo Benegas, Eric Czech

Open Athena, New York, NY

CSHL 90th Symposium · AI in Biology
May 26–31, 2026

Pretraining data composition is a known lever for language-model performance, but no study has systematically curated data for genomic language models. We train autoregressive Transformers from 6M to 1.7B parameters and vary two axes: functional regions of the genome and the evolutionary timescale of training species. Across ClinVar and TraitGym zero-shot variant-effect tasks, both axes matter — and well-curated small models can outrun much larger ones.

TraitGym promoter AUPRC — our 6M-parameter region-specific gLM beats the 40B-parameter Evo 2 baseline on zero-shot variant effect. smaller, focused training data > larger, undifferentiated training data.

A.Functional regions

R1

Region-specific small models beat a 40B baseline on promoters.

A 6M-parameter gLM trained on promoter sequences reaches 0.77 AUPRC on TraitGym promoter variant effect, versus 0.57 for the 40B-parameter Evo 2 trained on the whole genome. Specialisation, not scale, drives the gap.

R2

Balanced sampling fixes the naive-union failure mode.

A model trained on the naive union of CDS and promoter sequences performs poorly on promoters — they are less abundant and noisier. Balanced sampling across regions closes the gap without retraining region-specific models.

B.Evolutionary timescales

T1

Mammals are the right timescale for promoter models.

For promoters, training on mammalian genomes outperforms both narrower (primates) and broader (animals) timescales — a sweet spot between diversity and evolutionary relevance to human regulation.

T2

CDS prefers a wider timescale — animals beat mammals.

For coding sequences, the optimum shifts outward: training on animals (a longer evolutionary timescale) beats mammals. Coding regions evolve slowly enough that distant homologs still carry usable signal.

Models

  • Autoregressive Transformers, 6M – 1.7B parameters.
  • Region-specific and mixed-region variants.
  • Trained on per-timescale species subsets (primates → animals).

Data

  • Genome FASTA from species at varying evolutionary distance.
  • Region masks for CDS / promoter / UTR / ncRNA.
  • Balanced sampling vs naive union (R2).

Evaluation

  • Zero-shot variant effect prediction.
  • ClinVar + TraitGym (promoter / CDS subsets).
  • AUPRC on pathogenic-vs-benign and trait-associated-vs-control.

Takeaways

  • For gLMs, what you train on outranks how big you train: a 6M region-specific model beats a 40B whole-genome model on promoters.
  • Balanced sampling is the cheap way to get region-specific behaviour without paying for a separate model per region.
  • Region × timescale interacts: promoters favour mammals; CDS favours animals. Treat the training corpus as a curation decision, not a scale-up decision.
  • Future work: principled timescale selection, projection-based curation, cross-species transfer.
